Why Knowledge Gets Stuck Behind Walls

AM A. Morandi· 6 min read· May 2026

Every year, millions of research papers are published. Almost none of them are read by the people whose lives they could change. The knowledge exists — it's just behind a wall.

Some of those walls are obvious. A study that could help a patient understand their diagnosis sits behind a paywall that costs more than a week's groceries. A history that could reshape how a community sees itself is written in a journal no one outside the field has heard of.

Other walls are quieter. They're made of language — the dense, careful, hedged prose that scholarship rewards. They're made of distance: the sense that universities are a different country, with their own customs, and that you'd need a passport to enter.

Gates assume you'll come to them

The traditional model of "public engagement" often amounts to leaving the gate open. Publish the paper, post the lecture, and trust that the curious will find their way in. But a gate, however open, still asks people to make the journey alone — to cross unfamiliar ground and hope they belong on the other side.

A bridge does something a gate never can: it meets you where you are.

That's the difference KnowBridge is built around. Not waiting for people to arrive, but carrying the knowledge across — translating it into video, audio, and plain language, and into more than one language at that. Curiosity, after all, has no single mother tongue.

What bridges look like in practice

It can be a thirty-second film that turns a dense paper into a story you feel. It can be a podcast where a researcher explains their life's work the way they'd explain it to a friend. It can be a short article — like this one — that trades footnotes for clarity without trading away the truth.

None of this replaces the research. The science still comes first. Bridges simply make sure it doesn't end at the water's edge.
